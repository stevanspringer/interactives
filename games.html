Payoff Matrices: Thinking About Outcomes

Many interactions in nature and human society can be reduced to a simple question: should I cooperate or defect? Cooperating means contributing to a shared outcome — sharing food, warning others of a predator, helping a competitor. Defecting means acting in your own interest, taking benefits without paying the costs.

To analyse these interactions we use a payoff matrix. Imagine two players: an actor who chooses a strategy, and a recipient who also chooses a strategy. The matrix shows the actor's payoff for each combination of choices. The four cells represent every possible pairing: both cooperate, both defect, or one cooperates while the other defects. In evolutionary game theory, payoff represents reproductive fitness — strategies with higher payoffs leave more offspring and increase in frequency over time.

The four classic two-player games differ in how the payoffs of individual interactions are ordered — which strategies do best and worst depends on the game and on what the other player does. A strategy that, once common in a population, cannot be invaded by any rare alternative is called an evolutionarily stable strategy (ESS).

Playing Against the Population

In nature, animals do not play a single game — they interact repeatedly with many different partners. The matrix and lines show the same underlying payoffs: the chart extends each cell value across all possible population compositions, so you can see at a glance how the relative advantage of each strategy shifts as the mix of the population changes. The key question for evolution is: which strategy does better on average across all those encounters? When the population is mostly cooperators, the actor meets cooperators most of the time; when most individuals defect, defectors dominate encounters. The average payoff of each strategy therefore changes as the mix of cooperators and defectors in the population shifts — and evolution favours whichever strategy has higher fitness.

The slope of each payoff line, and whether they cross — and if so, where — determines how the population evolves in response to selection.
