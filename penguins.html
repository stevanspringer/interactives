Emperor Penguin Huddling

Thermoregulation and collective behaviour in Aptenodytes forsteri

Background

Emperor penguins (Aptenodytes forsteri) breed during the Antarctic winter, enduring temperatures as low as −60 °C and wind speeds exceeding 200 km h−1. To survive without food for months while incubating eggs, they form dense huddles — one of the most remarkable examples of collective behaviour in the animal kingdom. No penguin is in charge. No penguin knows the shape of the huddle. The structure emerges entirely from each individual following a few simple local rules.

Two Simple Rules

Each penguin has a temperature that rises when it is surrounded by neighbours and falls when it is exposed. Just two rules drive the collective behaviour:

1. Gain and lose heat locally. Each step a penguin gains +1 for every occupied neighbouring cell and loses −1 for every empty one. A penguin with three neighbours neither warms nor cools; with four or more it warms; with two or fewer it cools. Wind adds an extra penalty of −1 to −5 to any penguin with an empty cell on its windward side.

2. Move when cold, stay when warm. A penguin whose neighbourhood produces a net heat loss moves into an adjacent empty cell, preferring the cell that borders the most other penguins. A penguin gaining heat stays put. The threshold slider adjusts how demanding penguins are: raise it and they move even when marginally warm; lower it and they tolerate mild heat loss before moving.
