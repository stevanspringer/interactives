Rock-Paper-Scissors

Simulating frequency-dependent selection in non-transitive strategies.

Based on real populations studied by Barry Sinervo and his team at UC Santa Cruz.

A Natural Game of Rock-Paper-Scissors

The side-blotched lizard (Uta stansburiana) lives in the deserts of southwestern North America. In these lizards there are three alternative types of males, which you can recognise by their colour. Each type of male takes a different mating strategy:

Rascals (R) — lusty lotharios; they seduce females.
Partners (P) — lifelong lovers; they guard females.
Scrappers (S) — loathsome louts; they fight for females.

Interactions

We will model a population of lizards that interact with each other. The interactions between different strategies determine fitness. The interactions between side-blotched lizard males are like a game of rock-paper-scissors. Each interaction is a contest between two males — the winner mates and produces two offspring with his strategy, replacing the original pair:

Scrappers (S) beat Partners (P)

⇒S beats P⇒Scrappers are strong, and able to win fights against Partners.

Partners (P) beat Rascals (R)

⇒P beats R⇒Partners guard their mates, so Rascals don’t have a chance to sneak in.

Rascals (R) beat Scrappers (S)

⇒R beats S⇒Rascals can sneak in against Scrappers when they are away fighting for mates.

R beats S, S beats P, and P beats R — Rock-Paper-Scissors.

Simulation of a Lizard Population

The population model will help us understand how the fitness of each strategy depends on its frequency. Scientists use models to make predictions. In this case, we want to predict how the frequency of each of the three strategies will change over time. The answer depends on the parameters — the settings of the model. Different settings can cause the model to have different outcomes.

Make a Prediction!

Think about a game of rock-paper-scissors played by pairing up all individuals in a population. If Rock were very common, how well would Paper do? What about Scissors? If wins increase the frequency of a strategy and losses decrease it, can you predict how the population will change next generation?

Questions

1) What does each bouncing dot represent? Does the graph tell you which strategy is common and which is rare? What do the x-axis and y-axis represent?

2) What settings cause allele frequencies to change quickly or slowly? Why does the frequency of each strategy go up, and then down? Do allele frequencies have to reach a stable, unchanging state?

3) What exactly happens when one of the strategies is lost by genetic drift? What parameter would you change to prevent this? Can you predict in advance which strategy will fix at 100%?

4) What assumption does the model make about genotype and phenotype? What do you think would happen if populations were structured in space so that lizards could only interact with their immediate neighbours?
