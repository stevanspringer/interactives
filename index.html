Vampire Bat Reciprocity

Why giving blood can cost less than it gains — the non-zero-sum arithmetic of starvation.

The Starvation Curve

Common vampire bats (Desmodus rotundus) must consume blood every night. A bat that fails to feed begins losing body weight immediately, and will starve — unable to maintain body temperature — when its weight falls to roughly 75% of its pre-fed weight. From a full meal, starvation takes about 60 hours; for a bat that missed a night's feeding, it arrives much sooner.

Gerald Wilkinson (1984) measured this relationship by weighing bats as they fasted and plotting their body weight as a percentage of their pre-fed weight against the time remaining until starvation. The key feature of the resulting curve is its shape: it is strongly nonlinear, with each percentage point of body weight worth many more survival hours for a hungry bat than for a well-fed one.

Hours until starvation ≈ 31.1 × ln(weight% − 70) − 54.6

The Cost and Benefit of Sharing Blood

Because the starvation curve is nonlinear, the same volume of blood has a very different time value depending on where each bat sits on the curve. A well-fed bat that donates blood is giving it up at a point where each unit buys relatively little survival time — the curve is moving slowly and it has hours to spare. The hungry recipient gains that same blood at a point where each unit is worth many more hours — blood is precious when you are close to starvation. This creates a non-zero-sum interaction: the total hours of survival time across both bats can increase as a result of the transfer. The donor loses fewer hours than the recipient gains. This is why natural selection can favour sharing even between unrelated individuals — as long as the probability of future reciprocation is high enough to offset the immediate cost.

How Nonlinearity Creates a Non-Zero-Sum Game

The previous section showed that a well-fed donor loses fewer hours than a hungry recipient gains when they share the same volume of blood. But why does this asymmetry exist? It exists entirely because of the shape of the starvation curve. If the relationship between body weight and survival time were a straight line, then every percentage point of body weight would be worth the same number of hours regardless of condition — and sharing would always be a zero-sum game.

The concavity of Wilkinson's curve is what makes the arithmetic non-zero-sum. A concave curve means that the slope (hours gained per unit of weight) is steeper at low body weights than at high ones. The donor gives blood where the slope is gentle; the recipient receives it where the slope is steep. The same volume of blood therefore buys more time than it costs.
