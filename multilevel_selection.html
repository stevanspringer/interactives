Virulence and Transmission

Why pathogens are not always as harmful as they could be.

The Virulence–Transmission Trade-off

A pathogen that kills its host quickly gains nothing if it never reaches a new one. This is the central tension of virulence evolution: within any infected host, the most rapidly replicating strains outcompete slower ones — but strains that destroy the host too fast lose access to future hosts. The key question is whether the evolutionary interests of pathogen strains within a host are aligned with what is best for transmission between hosts.

This tension maps directly onto the logic of multilevel selection. Each infected host is a group. Blue strains are mild — they replicate at a moderate rate, leave the host functional, and support sustained transmission. Orange strains are virulent — they replicate faster within the host, outcompeting mild strains, but drive the host to collapse before transmission can occur. Within any single host, virulent strains always win the within-host competition. But hosts carrying only virulent strains die without transmitting, while hosts with mild strains survive and keep spreading the pathogen.

Whether mild strains persist in the pathogen population depends on which force is stronger: within-host competition, which always favours virulence, or between-host selection, which favours mild strains that preserve the host. The outcome depends critically on the relative rates of virulence and transmission.

Two Forces, One Pathogen Population

Virulence sets the rate at which virulent strains spread within a host, converting mild-strain infections into virulent ones. High virulence means rapid within-host competitive exclusion: mild strains are swept out before the host can transmit them.

Transmission sets the rate at which strains move between hosts. Low transmission keeps hosts isolated — within-host selection dominates, and virulence wins everywhere. High transmission constantly shuffles strains between hosts, mixing their compositions and erasing the between-host differences that allow mild strains to persist as a class. Intermediate transmission allows mild strains to colonise newly available hosts while preserving enough between-host variation for between-host selection to act.

When a host reaches carrying capacity and carries only virulent strains, it collapses — the virulent strains have exhausted the host. Hosts carrying mild strains remain stable at carrying capacity. Periodically, a virulent strain is introduced into a fully mild host population, restarting the within-host competition. Collapsed hosts are eventually recolonised by mild strains transmitted from surviving hosts.

Questions

Q1. Set transmission to zero and virulence to a high rate. What eventually happens to every host — and do all hosts collapse at the same time?

Q2. Now add a low transmission rate. Do mild strains persist longer globally, compared to zero transmission? Why?

Q3. Set transmission to very high. What happens to the composition of different hosts — and what does this mean for mild strains globally?

Q4. After a virulent strain enters a fully-mild host, what determines whether mild strains can survive within that host long enough to transmit before being competitively excluded?

Q5. What combination of virulence and transmission gives mild strains the best chance of persisting? Is there a sweet spot for transmission, or does maximising it always help?

Q6. In nature, many pathogens transmit before causing serious harm to the host. What does the model suggest about why this pattern is so common?
